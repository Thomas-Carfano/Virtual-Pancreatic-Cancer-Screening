# 11. Pancreatic Cancer Risk Factors — Hereditary and Modifiable

## 1. The Case for Risk-Based Screening

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is among the deadliest cancers, with an overall five-year survival under 13%. Roughly 80% of patients present at unresectable stages because the pancreas sits deep in the retroperitoneum and early disease is silent. Unlike breast, colon, cervical, lung, or prostate cancer — all of which have evidence-based population-screening programs — pancreatic cancer has *no* approved population-level screening. The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF) gave routine screening of asymptomatic adults a "D" recommendation in 2019, on the basis that the disease's low population incidence (~13 cases per 100,000 per year), the imperfect specificity of current tests, and the limited survival benefit from earlier detection together yield more harm than benefit. But this calculus changes dramatically when the absolute risk in a defined subgroup climbs above ~5–10%. About 10% of PDAC has a meaningful hereditary component, and a much larger fraction is attributable to modifiable exposures that compound with age and genotype. A risk-stratified strategy — Mendelian for high-penetrance genes, polygenic-plus-clinical for the rest, with new-onset diabetes (NOD) as an opportunistic warning signal — is the only screening framework currently supported by cost-effectiveness data. This document catalogs the evidence base for each risk factor in turn and flags where computational work could move the needle.

## 3. Hereditary Syndromes in Depth

### BRCA1 and BRCA2

The BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes encode key proteins in the homologous-recombination (HR) pathway, which repairs double-strand DNA breaks with high fidelity. Loss of either creates a tumor that is dependent on alternative, error-prone repair pathways — and that becomes the therapeutic foothold for PARP inhibitors, which trap PARP1 on DNA and force the cell to attempt HR repair it can no longer perform (synthetic lethality). The 2024 international prospective cohort estimated cumulative PDAC incidence from age 40–80 at 2.2% for BRCA1 and 2.7% for BRCA2 carriers, though older retrospective series put BRCA2 lifetime risk as high as 5–10%, with relative risks of 3–6. Approximately 5–10% of all PDAC cases carry a germline BRCA1/2 variant (higher — up to 19% — in Ashkenazi Jewish populations because of the three founder mutations 185delAG, 5382insC in BRCA1 and 6174delT in BRCA2, which collectively reach ~1-in-40 carrier frequency). The **POLO trial** established olaparib maintenance after first-line platinum-based chemotherapy in metastatic germline-BRCA-mutated PDAC, doubling progression-free survival (7.4 vs 3.8 months, HR 0.53), though without overall-survival benefit. Universal germline testing of all PDAC patients is now NCCN-recommended, principally to identify BRCA1/2 (and other HR genes) for PARP-inhibitor eligibility and for cascade testing of relatives.

### PALB2 (Partner And Localizer of BRCA2)

PALB2 is essential for BRCA2 localization to sites of DNA damage; biallelic loss causes Fanconi anemia, and heterozygous germline pathogenic variants behave functionally like BRCA2 deficits. PALB2 is the second-most-frequently-mutated gene in hereditary PDAC after BRCA2, accounting for 1–3% of PDAC. The PALB2 international consortium (524 families) reported a pancreatic-cancer relative risk of 2.4 (95% CI 1.24–4.50). Most surveillance programs treat PALB2 like BRCA2 — annual EUS/MRI if at least one affected FDR. Tumors are HR-deficient and respond to platinum and (likely) PARP inhibitors, though formal trial data are thinner than for BRCA1/2.

### ATM (Ataxia-Telangiectasia Mutated)

ATM is the master kinase activated by double-strand breaks, phosphorylating CHK2, p53, BRCA1, and dozens of other targets. Homozygous loss causes ataxia-telangiectasia; heterozygous germline pathogenic variants are present in ~2.3% of unselected PDAC patients — making ATM the **single most prevalent hereditary PDAC predisposition gene by absolute count**. Cumulative pancreatic-cancer risk by age 80 in pathogenic-ATM carriers is ~9.5% (vs. baseline 1.6%), with a relative risk of 5.7–6.5. ATM-deficient tumors may have HR-deficiency-like phenotypes; ATR/PARP combinations are under investigation. CAPS recommends surveillance for ATM carriers with at least one affected FDR.

### Lynch Syndrome (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2, EPCAM)

Lynch syndrome — defects in DNA mismatch-repair — produces tumors with microsatellite instability (MSI-high) and high tumor-mutational burden, conferring profound responsiveness to immune-checkpoint inhibitors (pembrolizumab). PDAC is an extracolonic Lynch tumor; cumulative risk by age 80 spans 6.2% for MLH1 to <1% for PMS2, with MSH2 (~1.5%) and MSH6 (~1.5%) intermediate. Although only 1% of PDAC overall, Lynch-associated PDAC is therapeutically distinct — MSI-high tumors should receive checkpoint inhibitor therapy. CAPS recommends surveillance for Lynch carriers with at least one affected FDR.

### FAMMM (Familial Atypical Multiple Mole Melanoma) / CDKN2A

CDKN2A encodes p16INK4A, an inhibitor of the CDK4/6 kinases that drive G1-to-S cell-cycle progression. Its loss is one of the four near-universal early somatic events in PDAC (along with KRAS, TP53, and SMAD4) — making germline loss especially catastrophic. The Dutch "p16-Leiden" founder mutation carries a **15–20% lifetime PDAC risk** (mean age at diagnosis ~58 years), with relative risks reported from 13- to 65-fold. CAPS recommends EUS/MRI annually **starting at age 40** regardless of family history. Patients should also undergo dermatologic melanoma surveillance.

### Peutz-Jeghers Syndrome (STK11 / LKB1)

STK11 / LKB1 is an upstream kinase of AMPK and a master metabolic regulator. Germline loss causes Peutz-Jeghers syndrome — mucocutaneous pigmentation, hamartomatous GI polyps, and one of the most penetrant cancer-predisposition syndromes known. The relative risk of pancreatic cancer in PJS is approximately **132–140-fold**, with cumulative risk reaching ~36% by age 60. The combined GI-tract cumulative cancer risk reaches 33% by age 60 and ~89% by age 65. CAPS recommends starting EUS/MRI annually at **age 30–40**.

### Hereditary Pancreatitis (PRSS1, SPINK1, CFTR, CTRC)

Gain-of-function mutations in PRSS1 (cationic trypsinogen) — most commonly R122H and N29I — cause autoactivation of trypsin within acinar cells, producing recurrent attacks of pancreatitis from childhood. SPINK1 (Kazal-type inhibitor) mutations are loss-of-function and require a second hit. Decades of inflammation create a permissive environment for malignant transformation: **cumulative pancreatic-cancer risk reaches ~40–53% by age 70 in PRSS1 carriers**, with smokers having further-elevated risk. CFTR mutations (both biallelic in cystic fibrosis and heterozygous carriers) are associated with idiopathic chronic pancreatitis and modestly elevated PDAC risk. Surveillance starts at age 40 or 20 years after the first pancreatitis attack.

### Li-Fraumeni Syndrome (TP53)

TP53 germline pathogenic variants cause a very broad cancer-predisposition syndrome with near-100% lifetime cancer risk. Although PDAC is not a "core" Li-Fraumeni cancer, carriers have ~7-fold increased PDAC risk over the general population. PDAC surveillance is not currently a formal CAPS recommendation for TP53 carriers without family history, but the question is under active study.

### Cystic Fibrosis / CFTR

Patients with cystic fibrosis have a ~5-fold elevated PDAC risk (small absolute number because cohort survival historically truncated cancer presentation). Heterozygous CFTR carriers — roughly 1 in 25 individuals of European ancestry — have a smaller risk increase (~1.5-fold) that becomes meaningful when combined with smoking or alcohol.

## 4. Familial Pancreatic Cancer Without an Identified Gene

The term **familial pancreatic cancer (FPC)** is reserved for kindreds with two or more affected first-degree relatives without an identified causal mutation. The Johns Hopkins National Familial Pancreas Tumor Registry data quantify the risk as **6.4-fold** with two affected FDRs (8–12% lifetime risk) and **32-fold** with three or more affected FDRs (~40% lifetime risk). Roughly 5–10% of all PDAC arises in FPC families, but in ~80% of these families no high-penetrance gene is identified after panel testing. Polygenic contributions (the additive effect of dozens of low-effect alleles identified through GWAS — e.g., ABO non-O, NR5A2, KLF5 loci) likely explain part of the residual familial aggregation, and rare-variant discovery using long-read sequencing or whole-genome approaches is ongoing. CAPS recommends EUS/MRI surveillance for FPC family members from age 50 (or 10 years younger than the youngest affected relative).

### Smoking — the dominant modifiable factor

A meta-analysis of 42 observational studies estimated **RR 1.8 (95% CI 1.7–1.9) for current smokers, 1.2 for former**, with non-linear dose response: RR ~1.5 at 10 cig/day, ~2.0 at 30 cig/day. Risk decays after cessation but takes ~15–20 years to normalize. The Global Burden of Disease project attributes ~21% of pancreatic-cancer deaths to smoking, and smoking cessation could eliminate up to 54% of PDAC in current smokers and ~16% overall. Mechanistically, tobacco-specific nitrosamines (NNK) cause KRAS codon-12 mutations — the founding genetic event in >90% of PDAC.

### Obesity and BMI

Per 5 kg/m² of BMI, summary RR is 1.06 (men) to 1.12 (women); obese individuals have RR ~1.19–1.47 vs. normal weight. Effect appears stronger in women. Adiposity drives insulin resistance and hyperinsulinemia (which stimulates IGF-1 receptor signaling on pancreatic cells), produces adipokine imbalance (leptin↑, adiponectin↓), and creates chronic low-grade inflammation. Visceral obesity (high waist-to-hip ratio) is a stronger driver than total BMI. PAF estimates: ~17% in general population, ~28% in obese subgroup.

### Alcohol

The dose-response meta-analyses converge on **RR ~1.15–1.22 for heavy drinking (≥3 drinks/day)**, with low-moderate intake showing no effect or possibly a slight inverse association. The strongest contribution is mediated through alcoholic chronic pancreatitis. Acetaldehyde is an IARC Group 1 carcinogen.

### Diet

Each 100 g/day of red meat increases PDAC risk ~11%; each 50 g/day of processed meat increases risk ~8–19%. Heme iron, N-nitroso compounds from nitrite curing, and heterocyclic amines from high-temperature cooking are the suspected mechanisms. Fruit and vegetable intake shows a modest inverse association.

### Chronic Pancreatitis

A nearly 8-fold increased PDAC risk at five years from chronic-pancreatitis diagnosis, with cumulative risk reaching ~4% at 15–20 years. Inflammation, acinar-ductal metaplasia, and PanIN progression are the proposed mechanisms.

### ABO Non-O Blood Group

Not modifiable but biologically interesting: non-O blood group (A, B, AB) carries OR ~1.23 (population PAF up to 17% because non-O is so common). The ABO locus encodes a glycosyltransferase modifying cell-surface sugars; non-O variants correlate with higher inflammatory markers (vWF, sICAM-1, TNF-α).

## 6. The Diabetes ↔ PDAC Bidirectional Relationship

The relationship between diabetes mellitus and PDAC is the most diagnostically actionable epidemiologic signal in pancreatic cancer. It is **bidirectional**:

1. **Long-standing T2DM (>3 years) → modestly elevated PDAC risk (RR 1.5–2.0).** This represents true causation — hyperinsulinemia and insulin resistance promote tumor initiation and growth via IGF-1, mTOR, and NF-κB signaling.

2. **New-onset diabetes (NOD) in adults ≥50 → 8-fold elevated risk of being diagnosed with PDAC within 3 years.** Roughly 0.5–1% of those meeting NOD criteria after age 50 will be diagnosed with PDAC in the next 3 years. This is **reverse causation** — a subclinical PDAC tumor is the *cause* of the diabetes, secreting paracrine factors (adrenomedullin, exosomal galectin-3, possibly others) that destroy β-cell function from a distance.

The clinical entity is sometimes termed **pancreatic-cancer-related diabetes (PCRD)** or "type 3c diabetes" when post-diagnosis. The distinguishing features vs. classic T2DM: weight *loss* (not gain), older age at onset, no family history of diabetes, and rapid metabolic deterioration.

**The ENDPAC score** (Enriching New-onset Diabetes for Pancreatic Cancer) stratifies NOD patients using three variables — age, change in weight, and change in fasting glucose at diabetes onset — into low (<0.1% 3-y PDAC risk), intermediate (~1.6%), and high (>3.6%) risk groups. ENDPAC ≥3 has shown sensitivity ~55% and specificity ~82% in independent validation, and is being prospectively studied in the NIH-funded **PANDOME** and **NOD-cohort screening studies**. The strategy: every NOD patient ≥50 gets ENDPAC scored; high-risk patients get CT/EUS imaging. If ~1% of NOD patients harbor PDAC and 25% of these are still resectable, this is one of the highest-yield screening opportunities in oncology.

## 7. The Microbiome and PDAC

### Oral Microbiome

Two large nested case-control studies (NCI-NIH cohorts) found that the presence of *Porphyromonas gingivalis* and *Aggregatibacter actinomycetemcomitans* in the oral microbiome — the same anaerobes that drive periodontitis — was associated with **a 59% increase in pancreatic-cancer risk**. Independently, individuals with high antibody titers against *P. gingivalis* (>200 ng/mL) had a **~2-fold higher PDAC risk**. Animal studies show *P. gingivalis* can translocate from the gingiva to the pancreas after gavage. Possible mechanisms: chronic systemic inflammation, induction of complement and TLR signaling, direct effects on pancreatic ductal cells.

### Periodontal Disease and Tooth Loss

Independent of the microbiome composition, clinical periodontitis and tooth loss confer ~RR 1.5–2.0 for PDAC across cohort studies. Whether periodontal-disease treatment lowers PDAC risk is an unresolved question and a tractable target for a randomized prevention trial.

### Gut Microbiome (fecal)

Pancreatic-cancer patients show increased *Fusobacterium nucleatum*, *Granulicatella adiacens*, and reduced overall diversity vs. controls. Whether dysbiosis is cause or consequence is unresolved.

### Intra-tumoral Microbiome

A 2017 *Science* paper (Geller et al.) showed that **76% of PDAC tumors harbor bacteria — predominantly Gammaproteobacteria (Klebsiella, Enterobacter)** — and that these bacteria express a long isoform of cytidine deaminase that converts gemcitabine into its inactive metabolite, **conferring chemotherapy resistance**. Co-treatment with ciprofloxacin restored gemcitabine activity in mouse models. A 2019 *Cell* paper (Riquelme et al.) showed that tumor microbiome diversity correlates with patient survival — long-term PDAC survivors have richer, more diverse intra-tumoral microbiomes than short-term survivors. *Fusobacterium nucleatum* in tumor tissue is associated with worse prognosis and is mechanistically linked to CXCL1-CXCR2 axis activation. A dissenting 2024 study reported low microbial biomass in tumor tissue and questioned the reproducibility of microbiome-prognosis associations; the question is not yet settled.

### Other Infectious Agents

- **Hepatitis B**: HR 1.5–1.53 for chronic infection; mechanism likely systemic inflammation.
- **Hepatitis C**: OR ~1.26; weaker but consistent signal.
- **Helicobacter pylori**: Mixed evidence (OR 1.09–1.45 across studies). The CagA-negative strains may be the risk-conferring ones; CagA-positive may be neutral or protective.

## 8. Current High-Risk Surveillance Protocols

### CAPS Consortium (2020 update)

The International Cancer of the Pancreas Screening Consortium issued consensus surveillance recommendations in 2013, 2020, and an additional 2024 cross-sectional survey through PRECEDE. The 2020 CAPS guidelines define eligible high-risk individuals (HRI):

- All carriers of **CDKN2A** (FAMMM) and **STK11** (Peutz-Jeghers), regardless of family history. **Start age 40.**
- Carriers of **BRCA1, BRCA2, PALB2, ATM, MLH1, MSH2, MSH6** with at least one affected first-degree relative. **Start age 45–50** (or 10 years younger than the youngest affected relative).
- **Familial pancreatic cancer** kindreds: first-degree relatives with at least 2 affected FDRs. **Start age 50.**
- **Hereditary pancreatitis** (PRSS1): start age 40 or 20 years after pancreatitis onset.

**Modality**: EUS and MRI/MRCP are both acceptable. Many programs alternate annually (EUS one year, MRI the next) or perform both at baseline and then alternate. Annual frequency is consensus in the absence of findings; 3–6 month intervals for indeterminate lesions; surgical referral for solid masses or high-grade-dysplasia IPMN.

**Targets**: T1N0M0 PDAC or high-grade dysplastic precursors (PanIN-3, IPMN with high-grade dysplasia).

### PRECEDE (Pancreatic Cancer Early Detection Consortium)

Launched in 2020, PRECEDE is a prospective multi-institutional cohort study aiming to enroll **>10,000** high-risk individuals across 7 cohorts stratified by family history and germline status. By late 2022, 3,402 participants were enrolled; ~52% in the highest-risk cohort (Cohort 1). PRECEDE is harmonizing imaging protocols, biospecimen banking, and longitudinal outcomes — providing the prospective dataset needed to refine surveillance recommendations and validate liquid-biopsy biomarkers in true high-risk populations. PRECEDE published a 2024 cross-sectional survey of institutional practices, finding wide variability in eligibility criteria (especially for BRCA1/2, ATM, PALB2 carriers without affected FDRs), an area of active controversy.

### Cost-Effectiveness Data

A 2019 Mayo Clinic analysis: MRI surveillance is cost-effective for individuals with 5-fold relative risk; EUS becomes dominant at >20-fold risk. A 2023 *JCO Oncology Practice* analysis found surveillance cost-effective at willingness-to-pay $50–100K/QALY for individuals with **lifetime risk ≥10%**. Combined MRI/EUS may be cost-effective at RR >12. Once age 76 is reached, no further screening is cost-effective.

## 9. Risk-Prediction Models

### PancPRO (Klein et al., 2007)

A Mendelian-based model extending the BRCAPRO framework. Inputs are family history of pancreatic and other cancers; outputs are absolute pancreatic-cancer risk and carrier probabilities. Validated in 961 NFPTR families with AUROC 0.75 and observed/expected ratio 0.83. Used clinically for genetic counseling.

### Klein Absolute Risk Model (2013, PLoS One)

A general-population model from PanScan data (3,349 cases / 3,654 controls). Inputs: current smoking (OR 2.20), heavy alcohol (1.45), obesity (1.26), diabetes >3y (1.57–1.80), family history (1.60), non-O ABO (1.23). Designed to identify the upper tail of the general-population risk distribution for potential intervention.

### ENDPAC (Sharma et al., 2018)

Three-variable model for new-onset diabetes patients ≥50: age, weight change, glucose change. Stratifies into <0.1%, 1.6%, >3.6% three-year PDAC risk. Sensitivity ~55%, specificity ~82%.

### BRCAPANCPRO

Mendelian model that integrates BRCA1/2 carrier probability with family history of breast, ovarian, and pancreatic cancers — useful for hereditary-breast-ovarian families where pancreatic risk is the secondary consideration.

### Deep Learning on EHR (Placek et al., *Nature Medicine* 2023)

A deep-learning model trained on EHR disease-trajectory data from 6 million Danish patients (24,000 PDAC cases) and validated on 3 million U.S. patients (3,900 cases). Predicts PDAC up to 36 months ahead from ICD-code sequences. Top features include known and novel risk patterns; the model identifies an actionable enriched cohort (10–50× general-population risk) at scale.

### Other ML Approaches

Systematic reviews of 30+ studies show that XGBoost, random forests, and multilayer perceptrons applied to EHR data outperform traditional logistic regression. The combination of structured EHR (ICDs, labs, meds) with unstructured notes via transformer-based LLMs is the active frontier.

## 10. Population-Level Screening — Case For and Against

### Case Against (current consensus, USPSTF "D")

- **Low population prevalence** (~13/100,000/year U.S.). Even a test with 99% specificity produces overwhelming false positives.
- **No proven survival benefit** at general-population screening — CA 19-9 has poor specificity (~75%), elevates in many benign biliary/inflammatory conditions, and is negative in Lewis-blood-group-negative individuals.
- **Imaging harms**: EUS is invasive (rare pancreatitis, sedation risk). MRI/MRCP yields incidental cysts in 10–20% of older adults, triggering further work-up.
- **Surgical harms**: Whipple procedures performed for benign or premalignant disease carry 30-day mortality ~2% and major morbidity ~25%.
- **Lead-time and length-time bias** may overstate apparent survival gain.

### Case For (or for risk-enriched screening)

- 80% present unresectable; resection is the only curative modality.
- 5-year survival for stage I PDAC is ~40%; for stage IV is <3%. Stage-shifting is the only obvious path to massive mortality reduction.
- High-penetrance genetic groups and NOD cohorts shift absolute risk into the cost-effective range.
- Liquid biopsy (ctDNA, methylation, multi-cancer-early-detection assays like Galleri) and AI on EHR offer plausible paths to enrich the population to be screened — making the *combined* strategy (risk-enrichment then imaging) potentially cost-effective at the general-population level within 5–10 years.

The pragmatic synthesis: **stratified screening, not blanket screening**. Identify the top ~1–5% of the population by absolute risk (via EHR-based ML + germline testing where indicated + NOD trigger), then deploy imaging surveillance only in that enriched group.

## 11. Where Compute Could Help — Tagged Items

The following are tractable computational projects directly relevant to PDAC risk and early detection:

- **[A] Polygenic risk score (PRS) refinement for PDAC.** Existing GWAS identify ~20 loci (ABO, NR5A2, KLF5, TERT, BCAR1, HNF1A/B, others). Combining PRS with rare-variant burden and clinical risk into a unified hybrid score requires large-scale population genetic computation. Cohort sample sizes are now sufficient (PanScan 4, UK Biobank, FinnGen, BioVU).
- **[A] EHR-based deep-learning risk prediction at scale.** The Placek 2023 *Nature Medicine* model used 6 million patients; replication and continuous-learning pipelines on additional national EHR datasets (Veterans Affairs, All of Us, Optum) would benefit from distributed computing. Federated-learning approaches across institutions are particularly compute-hungry.
- **[A] Polygenic + clinical + family history integrated risk model.** A "BRCAPRO-style" Mendelian model joined with a polygenic score and Klein clinical features, plus an EHR ML predictor, ensembled. Bayesian integration is the natural framework.
- **[A] In-silico screening of PARP and ATR inhibitor combinations for HR-deficient PDAC.** Molecular dynamics and free-energy perturbation calculations on PARP1, ATR, ATM, BRCA1/2 binding pockets. BOINC-amenable.
- **[A] Microbiome metagenomic computation.** Mapping oral, gut, and intra-tumoral microbiome data against PDAC risk and treatment response. K-mer-based and assembly-based pipelines on petabyte-scale sequence data.
- **[A] Liquid-biopsy methylation signal extraction.** Whole-genome bisulfite sequencing of cell-free DNA produces 100GB+ per patient; calling methylation signatures and tissue-of-origin signals is compute-intensive.
- **[A] Cost-effectiveness Markov simulations** of risk-stratified screening at scale — millions of synthetic patients across thousands of policy parameterizations.
- **[A] Synthetic-data generation for rare hereditary syndromes** (e.g., FAMMM, Peutz-Jeghers) to train ML models robust on small real-world cohorts.
- **[A] Virtual screening of ATM/ATR allosteric inhibitors** to expand the synthetic-lethality therapeutic window for ATM-mutant PDAC.
- **[A] PanIN/IPMN-to-PDAC progression modeling** — agent-based simulations of clonal evolution under different surveillance intervals to inform optimal screening frequency.
