# 12. Pancreatic Cancer — Clinical Presentation, Diagnosis, and Staging

## 1. Why Diagnosis Is So Hard

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is the textbook example of a "silent until late" cancer. The pancreas sits deep in the retroperitoneum, draped behind the stomach and tucked between the duodenum, spleen, aorta, and the great mesenteric vessels. Nothing palpates it, nothing tests it directly, and small tumors do not deform any structure a patient or clinician can feel. Symptoms appear when a tumor either obstructs the common bile duct (which only happens for tumors in the *head* of the pancreas, and only once they reach a centimeter or two), invades the celiac plexus posteriorly (causing back pain), disrupts pancreatic exocrine or endocrine function broadly enough to be clinically obvious, or seeds the peritoneum and liver. By the time any of these things happens, the tumor has typically been growing — and in many cases metastasizing — for years. The Cleveland Clinic puts it bluntly: "there aren't any early signs of pancreatic cancer; symptoms often start once the tumor impacts other organs." The result is that roughly 80% of patients present at unresectable stages, and median symptom-to-diagnosis interval still exceeds two months in most healthcare systems. Every component of the diagnostic workup discussed below is, in effect, a workaround for the basic problem that there is no early-warning physical sign and no validated population-screening test.

## 2. Symptoms by Anatomic Location

The pancreas is divided anatomically into **head** (right of the superior mesenteric vessels, hugging the duodenum), **neck**, **body** (overlying the spine), and **tail** (extending into the splenic hilum). Roughly **60–70% of PDACs arise in the head**, **15–20% in the body**, and **10–15% in the tail** — though obesity is associated with distal migration. Because the head sits adjacent to the bile duct and duodenum, head tumors produce obstructive symptoms earlier and are therefore diagnosed at smaller average size than body/tail tumors, which can grow silently to large dimensions before producing systemic effects.

### 2.1 Head-of-Pancreas Tumors

**Painless jaundice and the Courvoisier sign.** The single most characteristic presentation is *painless* obstructive jaundice. The intrapancreatic portion of the common bile duct passes through the pancreatic head; a tumor of even 1–2 cm at that location can compress it, blocking the flow of bile into the duodenum. Conjugated bilirubin spills back into the bloodstream, staining the sclerae and skin yellow. Patients also notice dark, tea-colored urine (bilirubin excreted by the kidneys), pale or clay-colored stools (no bilirubin reaching the gut to be converted to brown stercobilin), and intense pruritus (itching) from bile-salt deposition in skin. The classic physical finding is **Courvoisier's sign**: a palpable, non-tender, distended gallbladder in the presence of painless jaundice. Ludwig Courvoisier proposed in 1890 that a gallbladder distended in this way usually points to a malignant cause (most often pancreatic head or ampullary cancer) rather than gallstones — because gallstones cause chronic gallbladder scarring and a contracted, fibrotic gallbladder, whereas a slowly developing malignant obstruction allows the gallbladder to passively distend with bile. Modern series put Courvoisier's specificity for malignancy at 83–90% but sensitivity at only 26–55%. Painless jaundice plus palpable gallbladder is present in 50–70% of patients with periampullary cancer or carcinoma of the head of pancreas.

**Other head-tumor symptoms.** Duodenal obstruction produces early satiety, nausea, and vomiting (often late). Gastric outlet obstruction occurs in 10–25%. Acute pancreatitis, when the tumor obstructs the main pancreatic duct, occurs in 5–10% — and any unexplained episode of pancreatitis in an adult over 40 without gallstones or alcohol exposure warrants imaging to exclude an obstructing tumor.

### 2.2 Body and Tail Tumors

Body and tail tumors lack a nearby bile duct to obstruct, so they generally announce themselves only by:

- **Persistent dull mid-epigastric pain that radiates to the back**, often worsened by lying flat and relieved by sitting forward, reflecting posterior invasion into the retroperitoneal nerve plexuses.
- **Profound weight loss and asthenia** out of proportion to dietary changes.
- **New or rapidly worsening diabetes** (see Section 2.4).
- **Splenic vein thrombosis** with sinistral (left-sided) portal hypertension — splenomegaly, isolated gastric varices, gastrointestinal bleeding — if the tail invades the splenic vein.

By the time these symptoms become unmistakable, body/tail tumors are typically larger and more advanced than head tumors at diagnosis, partly explaining their worse stage-stratified outcomes.

### 2.3 Back and Abdominal Pain — The Anatomy

The pain of PDAC is one of the most debilitating features of the disease. The mechanism is not stretch of the pancreatic capsule alone. Rather, **perineural invasion is found in 80–100% of resected PDACs** — the tumor preferentially tracks along sympathetic and parasympathetic nerve fibers, invading the nerve sheaths themselves. The pancreas is innervated by the celiac and superior mesenteric plexuses (T5-T12 sympathetic input, vagal parasympathetic input), and tumor cells reach the celiac ganglion via direct extension or perineural spread. Neuropathic pain dominates in the late stages (~70–90% of pain). The result is a deep, gnawing, often unrelenting mid-epigastric pain that bores through to the back at the T10-L1 level, occasionally band-like and worsened by meals or supine posture. Celiac-plexus neurolysis (chemical block with alcohol, usually under EUS guidance) is the most effective palliative intervention for refractory pain.

### 2.4 New-Onset Diabetes

About **one in four PDAC patients develops diabetes within the 36 months preceding diagnosis**. New-onset diabetes (NOD) in an adult over 50 increases the 3-year pancreatic-cancer probability to roughly **0.8–1.0%, six- to ten-fold the population baseline**. The mechanism is paraneoplastic: tumor-secreted factors (proposed candidates include adrenomedullin, exosome-borne miRNAs, vanin-1, S100A8/A9, and components of the metabolomic signature in NOD-PC patients) induce both peripheral insulin resistance and beta-cell dysfunction. Two features distinguish PDAC-associated NOD from ordinary type 2 diabetes: (1) **weight loss accompanies hyperglycemia** rather than obesity, and (2) **glucose control worsens despite progressive weight loss** — the opposite of how type 2 diabetes typically behaves. The "Enriching New-Onset Diabetes for Pancreatic Cancer" (END-PAC) score and similar tools attempt to flag higher-risk NOD subsets for imaging. Whether to screen all NOD patients over 50 is an open question — most do not have cancer, but the population-attributable risk concentration is large enough that focused, biomarker-triggered screening is being actively studied.

### 2.5 Trousseau Syndrome — Migratory Thrombophlebitis

In 1865 Armand Trousseau described migratory superficial venous thrombosis as an occult-cancer marker; he later recognized the sign in himself and correctly predicted his own gastric cancer. PDAC accounts for **~50% of all Trousseau-syndrome cases** among solid tumors. The mechanism is a tumor-induced hypercoagulable state: mucinous adenocarcinomas (and PDAC is the prototype) shed sialylated mucins that interact with P- and L-selectins on platelets and leukocytes, triggering platelet activation. The tumor also overexpresses tissue factor (the initiator of the extrinsic coagulation cascade), releases procoagulant microparticles, activates the contact pathway via cysteine proteases, and induces endothelial dysfunction. Clinically, this presents as recurrent, anatomically wandering ("migratory") superficial venous thrombi — calf today, forearm next week — sometimes accompanied by deep-vein thrombosis, pulmonary embolism, or nonbacterial thrombotic endocarditis. Up to 20–30% of PDAC patients develop a clinically significant venous thromboembolism somewhere in their disease course, the highest rate of any solid tumor.

### 2.6 Steatorrhea and Exocrine Insufficiency

Steatorrhea — fatty, pale, foul-smelling stools that float and are difficult to flush — results from inadequate delivery of pancreatic lipase, colipase, and other digestive enzymes to the duodenum. The pancreas normally secretes a vast functional reserve (lipase output must drop to under ~10% of normal before fat malabsorption manifests), so symptomatic exocrine insufficiency reflects either bulk replacement of acinar tissue by tumor, obstruction of the main pancreatic duct, or post-resection loss of parenchyma. Up to **66–92% of patients with unresectable PDAC have pancreatic exocrine insufficiency (PEI)** by enzymatic testing, though only ~16.7% have classic steatorrhea — many present more subtly with bloating, flatulence, undigested-food appearance in stool, fat-soluble vitamin (A/D/E/K) deficiency, and progressive weight loss. PERT (pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy with pancrelipase, dosed at 40,000–50,000 lipase units per main meal) is recommended for every patient with confirmed PEI regardless of prognosis, and likely under-prescribed in routine oncology practice.

### 2.7 Depression as a Presenting Symptom

PDAC has the **highest incidence of comorbid depression of any digestive-system cancer**, and depression *precedes* the cancer diagnosis in up to ~50% of patients — sometimes by months. This is too consistent to be entirely "reactive depression to occult illness"; the biology points toward tumor-driven inflammatory cytokines (especially IL-6) crossing the blood-brain barrier and dysregulating the HPA axis, plus tumor production of neurotransmitter-like substances and a possible neuroendocrine paraneoplastic component akin to limbic encephalitis. Clinically, this means **new depression in an adult over 50 without an obvious psychosocial trigger** should at least raise the question of an unrecognized abdominal malignancy, especially if accompanied by weight loss, anorexia, vague abdominal discomfort, or new diabetes.

### 2.9 Differential Diagnosis of the Pancreatic Mass / Painless Jaundice

## 3. PDAC-Specific Cachexia — Major Morbidity Driver

Cachexia (Greek: *kakos hexis*, "bad condition") is a syndrome of involuntary weight loss with depletion of skeletal muscle (sarcopenia) and adipose tissue, accompanied by anorexia, asthenia, anemia, and an elevated inflammatory tone. It is present at diagnosis in roughly **80% of PDAC patients** and ultimately becomes a contributing cause of death in an estimated **30%**. PDAC cachexia is *not* simple starvation; it cannot be reversed by feeding alone. It is a tumor-driven metabolic catastrophe with overlapping endocrine, inflammatory, and tissue-crosstalk mechanisms.

**IL-6 trans-signaling.** Tumor cells secrete IL-6, which acts on muscle and adipose tissue via the classical (membrane IL-6 receptor) and trans-signaling (soluble IL-6R / gp130) pathways. In murine PDAC, *deleting IL-6 from the tumor alone* halves adipose wasting and abolishes myosteatosis (fat infiltration of muscle) and muscle atrophy — establishing IL-6 as the keystone signal. In adipose tissue, IL-6 induces lipolysis and "browning" (a thermogenic, energy-wasting transition mediated by UCP1). In muscle, IL-6 activates STAT3 signaling and the ubiquitin-proteasome system (atrogin-1 / MuRF-1), driving myofibrillar protein degradation. Crosstalk between depots amplifies the signal: muscle releases soluble IL-6R that potentiates trans-signaling in fat; fat releases free fatty acids and additional IL-6 that further worsen muscle wasting.

**GDF15 / GFRAL anorexia.** Growth differentiation factor 15 (GDF15), a divergent TGF-β superfamily member, is elevated in tumor tissue and serum of PDAC patients. GDF15 binds GFRAL receptors on hindbrain neurons (area postrema and nucleus tractus solitarius), triggering nausea, food aversion, and reduced caloric intake. This is the dominant mechanism of cancer-associated anorexia and is now a therapeutic target: anti-GFRAL antibodies (ponsegromab from Pfizer) are in Phase II trials and have shown meaningful weight gain in PDAC-cachexia cohorts.

**Other contributors.** Tumor-induced glucagon-like signaling, fasting-state insulin resistance, suppressed ketogenesis via IL-6/STAT3 (which paradoxically *prevents* the patient's body from using fat stores efficiently in early disease), Activin A / myostatin signaling, and exocrine insufficiency-driven malabsorption all combine. The result is a metabolic state in which the patient is simultaneously catabolic at the muscle/fat level *and* unable to access stored energy effectively *and* anorexic — a triple loss.

Cachexia matters for diagnosis because (a) profound weight loss is itself a presenting symptom that prompts imaging, (b) sarcopenia and myosteatosis on diagnostic CT (now routinely quantifiable from the L3 vertebral slice) are independent prognostic markers and predict chemotherapy tolerance, and (c) it modifies the entire downstream treatment plan — sarcopenic patients tolerate FOLFIRINOX poorly and may benefit from neoadjuvant prehabilitation.

## 4. Initial Laboratory Workup and Tumor Markers

The first-line laboratory workup on a patient presenting with suspected PDAC or with painless jaundice / unexplained weight loss is intentionally broad — partly diagnostic, partly to characterize biliary obstruction, liver function, coagulation, and nutritional status before any intervention.

| Test | What it shows in PDAC | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **CBC** | Often normal early; mild anemia possible (chronic disease or occult GI bleed); thrombocytosis can be paraneoplastic | — |
| **CMP / LFTs** | If biliary obstruction: ↑ direct bilirubin, ↑ alkaline phosphatase, ↑ GGT, modest ↑ AST/ALT; ↓ albumin, ↑ glucose | "Cholestatic pattern" |
| **Lipase / amylase** | Often *normal*; ↑ if tumor obstructs main duct → acute pancreatitis | Negative result does *not* exclude PDAC |
| **INR / PT** | Prolonged from fat-soluble vitamin K malabsorption in obstructive jaundice; corrects with parenteral vitamin K | Important before any intervention |
| **HbA1c / fasting glucose** | Elevated in ~25–50%; can be the first abnormality | NOD red flag in >50yo + weight loss |
| **CA 19-9** | Elevated in ~70–80% at diagnosis | See detailed discussion below |
| **CEA** | Elevated in ~40–60%; less specific than CA 19-9 | Mainly used in combination |
| **IgG4** | To rule in/out autoimmune pancreatitis (Type 1) | Especially with diffuse pancreatic mass |
| **Hepatitis serologies, ferritin, ceruloplasmin** | Per local workup of jaundice | — |

### 4.1 CA 19-9 in Depth

CA 19-9 (sialyl Lewis-a) is a sialylated glycan attached to mucins and is the most widely used serum tumor marker for PDAC. Its performance, however, has well-characterized limits:

- **Sensitivity 79–81%, specificity 82–90%** for symptomatic patients at the standard cutoff of 37 U/mL.
- **Not validated for asymptomatic screening** — the positive predictive value at the population baseline pancreatic-cancer prevalence (~0.013% / year) is below 1%, so the test produces overwhelmingly more false positives than true cases.
- **False positives** are routine in any cause of cholestasis: gallstones, cholangitis, primary sclerosing cholangitis, autoimmune pancreatitis, chronic pancreatitis, cirrhosis, and even some benign biliary strictures can drive CA 19-9 well above 1,000 U/mL. Decompressing the biliary tree (stenting) often produces a sharp fall — a useful clinical observation, though it complicates interpretation.
- **Lewis-negative patients** (~5–10% of Caucasians, higher in some populations) lack the FUT3 fucosyltransferase needed to synthesize the Lewis-a backbone and therefore produce **little or no CA 19-9 regardless of tumor burden**. In one serial study, 91.7% of Lewis-negative patients had undetectable CA 19-9 throughout their disease course; only 27.4% of Lewis-negative PDAC patients ever crossed the 37 U/mL cutoff. Lewis-negative status is heritable and a known blind spot for any CA 19-9-based detection scheme.
- **Prognostic and treatment-response use** is more robust than diagnostic use: a CA 19-9 over 1,000 U/mL at diagnosis correlates with metastatic disease and predicts a higher rate of positive staging laparoscopy; failure of CA 19-9 to fall by ≥50% after biliary decompression or after neoadjuvant chemotherapy is a poor prognostic sign; rising CA 19-9 during follow-up often heralds recurrence by months.

### 4.2 Newer Biomarker Panels

The known limitations of CA 19-9 have driven a generation of replacement and adjunct biomarker work. Key panels in the 2024–2026 landscape include:

| Panel / Test | Components | Reported performance | Use case |
|---|---|---|---|
| **CA 19-9 + MUC5AC** | Mucin secreted by PDAC but not benign pancreas | AUC 0.894 (vs. 0.81 CA 19-9 alone), Sens 73.8% / Spec 88.6% | Complement to CA 19-9 |
| **CA 19-9 + MUC5AC + MUC16** | Detection of CA 19-9 antigen specifically on MUC5AC and MUC16 protein carriers (PAULA's test) | 67–80% sens at 98% spec; rescues some Lewis-negative cases | Adjunct in screening high-risk cohorts |
| **CA 19-9 + TIMP1 + LRG1** | Tissue inhibitor of metalloprotease-1 + Leucine-rich α2-glycoprotein | AUC 0.949 / 0.887 in validation; 85% / 67% sens at 95% spec | Early-stage PDAC vs. healthy / benign |
| **CancerSEEK (Exact Sciences)** | Multi-analyte: 16 mutation gene panel + 8 protein biomarkers (CA 19-9, CA-125, CEA, HGF, OPN, prolactin, TIMP-1, myeloperoxidase) | Pan-cancer ~70% sens overall; pancreas ~72% but population-screening positive predictive value unsettled | Pan-cancer multimodal; FDA Breakthrough designation |
| **Galleri (GRAIL)** | Methylated cell-free DNA across >100,000 CpG sites | Stage I/II PDAC ~61% sens (lower than IMMray PanCan-d); pan-cancer ~52% sens at 99.5% spec | Pan-cancer methylation; available as LDT |
| **IMMray PanCan-d (Immunovia)** | 8-protein biomarker signature + CA 19-9 in proprietary algorithm | Stage I/II PDAC ~89–92% sens at 99% spec in high-risk validation | High-risk surveillance (FPC, BRCA, etc.); withdrawn from US market in 2024 amid commercial restructuring |

Each panel has different operating characteristics, and none is currently approved for asymptomatic population screening. For a project that aims at *earlier* PDAC detection, the bottleneck is not catalog discovery (markers exist) but cost-effective deployment in defined high-risk cohorts plus, critically, robust handling of the Lewis-negative population that the CA 19-9 backbone cannot serve.

## 5. Imaging — CT, MRI/MRCP, EUS, ERCP, PET

### 5.1 Pancreatic-Protocol CT — The Gold Standard

Multi-phase contrast-enhanced CT performed with a "pancreatic protocol" is the cornerstone diagnostic study for any suspected pancreatic mass. The protocol acquires images in three or four phases after iodinated contrast:

1. **Non-contrast** (sometimes omitted) — to detect calcifications and characterize hemorrhage.
2. **Late arterial / pancreatic-parenchymal phase** (~35–45 seconds post-bolus) — provides the **best contrast between hypoenhancing PDAC and the surrounding bright pancreatic parenchyma** and is the optimum window for detecting small tumors.
3. **Portal venous phase** (~65–75 seconds) — best for detecting liver metastases and characterizing the relationship between tumor and adjacent vessels (SMV, portal vein).
4. **Delayed phase** (optional, ~3–5 minutes) — characterizes some fibrotic and inflammatory mimics.

Thin reconstructions (0.5–1.0 mm slices), multiplanar reformatting, and curved-multiplanar reformats along the pancreatic duct and adjacent vasculature are standard. Reported sensitivity for PDAC at experienced centers is 89–97% overall; **detection drops to ~70% for tumors under 2 cm**, which is a major rationale for adding EUS in equivocal cases.

The pancreatic-protocol CT is also the basis for the **resectability assessment** (Section 9) — the radiologist explicitly grades degree of contact with SMA, celiac axis, common hepatic artery, SMV, and portal vein.

### 5.2 MRI / MRCP

MRI with magnetic-resonance cholangiopancreatography (MRCP) is complementary, not redundant. It is preferred when:

- The patient has an iodinated-contrast allergy or impaired renal function.
- A pancreatic-protocol CT is equivocal — especially for **small (<2 cm) tumors**, which MRI may detect with comparable or slightly higher sensitivity due to its superior soft-tissue contrast.
- **Detailed ductal anatomy** is needed (MRCP is non-invasive, fluid-sensitive imaging of the bile and pancreatic ducts, producing the same anatomic information as a diagnostic ERCP without the intervention).
- **Cystic lesions** require characterization (T2-weighted sequences and MRCP delineate cyst-duct communication, crucial for IPMN diagnosis).
- **Liver metastases** are equivocal on CT (MRI with hepatobiliary contrast such as gadoxetate disodium is the most sensitive test for sub-centimeter liver lesions).

### 5.3 Endoscopic Ultrasound (EUS) — and Tissue Sampling

EUS places a high-frequency ultrasound transducer at the tip of an endoscope, advancing it through the stomach and duodenum to image the pancreas through the gastric/duodenal wall — a few centimeters of soft tissue rather than 30 cm of abdominal wall and bowel gas. It is the most sensitive test available for small (<2 cm) pancreatic masses and the only modality that allows simultaneous **tissue sampling**.

- **EUS-FNA (fine-needle aspiration)** — 22- or 25-gauge needle producing cytologic material.
- **EUS-FNB (fine-needle biopsy)** — newer-design 19- or 22-gauge cutting needles (Franseen, Fork-tip, ProCore) producing core histologic specimens.

A 2024 meta-analysis of small (≤15 mm) pancreatic lesions reported **EUS-FNB diagnostic accuracy 89.8% vs. 79.1% for FNA**, with sample adequacy 95.9% vs. 86.1%, and importantly fewer passes needed (≤3 passes adequate in 94% of FNB vs. 39% of FNA). FNB is therefore preferred at most centers in 2026 and reduces the operational dependence on rapid on-site cytopathology evaluation (ROSE). Adverse-event rates remain low (~0.5–1% mild pancreatitis or bleeding).

EUS also allows fluid sampling of pancreatic cysts (CEA + amylase + cytology + molecular markers — see Section 10) and is the access point for therapeutic interventions (celiac plexus neurolysis, biliary or pancreatic ductal drainage in expert hands).

### 5.4 ERCP — Diagnostic Role Has Shrunk

Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP), once the diagnostic procedure of choice, is now reserved primarily for **therapeutic biliary stenting** in obstructive jaundice. Its diagnostic role has been displaced by MRCP (non-invasive) and EUS (better tissue sampling, no risk of post-ERCP pancreatitis). ERCP-related pancreatitis occurs in ~3–5% of procedures (higher in difficult cannulations), and adverse events outweigh the diagnostic information available from non-invasive alternatives. The current ERCP indications in PDAC are:

- Drainage of malignant biliary obstruction (plastic stent for short-term decompression pre-surgery, self-expanding metal stent for definitive palliation).
- Brushings for cytology in indeterminate biliary strictures (yield 30–60%).
- Endoscopic management of post-resection complications.

### 5.5 PET-CT — Targeted Adjunct, Not Primary

¹⁸F-FDG PET-CT shows variable PDAC avidity. It is *not* the primary diagnostic or staging modality, but it has a defined adjunctive role:

- **Detection of occult distant metastases** in patients with high pre-test probability (very elevated CA 19-9, borderline-resectable tumors, ambiguous CT findings) — changes management in ~10–16% of cases.
- **Resolution of equivocal CT findings** — especially indeterminate small liver lesions or distant lymphadenopathy.

Limitations are significant: lesions <5 mm are routinely missed (low metabolic state, partial volume averaging), lymph-node sensitivity is only 21–38%, and false positives occur with autoimmune pancreatitis, chronic pancreatitis flares, and post-procedural inflammation. PET is **not** a replacement for staging CT in initial workup.

### 5.6 Imaging Modality Comparison

| Modality | Best for | Key limitations | Sensitivity (PDAC) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pancreatic-protocol CT** | Initial diagnosis; resectability; metastases | <2 cm tumors; iodine contrast; radiation | 89–97% overall, 70–80% if <2 cm |
| **MRI / MRCP** | Small tumors; cysts; ductal anatomy; liver mets; contrast-allergic | Time, cost, motion artifact | ~90–95%; superior for liver mets with hepatobiliary contrast |
| **EUS ± FNB** | Small tumors; tissue sampling; cyst characterization | Operator-dependent; sedation; limited at body/tail tip | ~95–98% detection; ~89–95% FNB accuracy |
| **ERCP** | Therapeutic biliary stenting; cytology of strictures | Post-ERCP pancreatitis (3–5%); invasive | Diagnostic role largely replaced |
| **¹⁸F-FDG PET-CT** | Occult metastases; equivocal CT findings | <5 mm lesions; nodal staging poor; false positives in inflammation | 87% for primary; lower for staging |

## 6. Tissue Sampling and Pathology

### 6.1 When Is Tissue Required?

**For surgical candidates with a clearly resectable mass**, tissue diagnosis is not absolutely required pre-operatively if imaging is unambiguous and other causes (autoimmune pancreatitis) have been excluded — straight-to-surgery is acceptable. For all other situations (neoadjuvant therapy planning, borderline-resectable, locally advanced, metastatic, atypical imaging, possible mimics), **pre-treatment tissue is mandatory**: chemotherapy cannot ethically begin without histologic confirmation, and the cytology often surfaces an unexpected diagnosis (NET, lymphoma, autoimmune, metastasis from another primary).

### 6.2 H&E Histology

PDAC is characterized on H&E by:

- Infiltrative, irregular glands with abundant desmoplastic stroma (the dense fibrotic reaction is a defining feature and a major treatment-resistance mechanism).
- Glands set in muscle bundles or directly adjacent to large arteries or veins (a feature considered diagnostic of malignancy in the pancreas).
- Perineural invasion (in essentially all cases at resection; often visible on small biopsies).
- Necrosis of duct contents ("luminal necrosis").
- Marked nuclear pleomorphism, prominent nucleoli, loss of polarity, and 4:1 or greater nuclear size variation within a single gland.

### 6.3 Immunohistochemistry (IHC) Panel

The standard "pancreatobiliary" IHC panel resolves uncertainty about origin in metastatic disease and helps exclude mimics:

| Marker | PDAC | Cholangiocarcinoma | Ampullary | Gastric | Colon | NET |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **CK7** | + | + | + | + (often) | – | Variable |
| **CK19** | + | + | + | + | + | + (often) |
| **CK20** | – or focal | – | + (intestinal type) | + | + | – |
| **CDX2** | – | – | + (intestinal type) | Variable | + | – |
| **MUC1** | + (apical, cytoplasmic) | + | + | Variable | – | – |
| **MUC2** | – | – | + (intestinal type) | – | + | – |
| **MUC5AC** | + | + | + (pancreatobiliary type) | + | – | – |
| **MUC6** | + (foveolar) | + | + | + | – | – |
| **Chromogranin A / Synaptophysin** | – | – | – | – | – | + |
| **SMAD4 (DPC4)** | Lost in ~55% | Retained | Retained | Retained | Retained | Retained |
| **p53** | Aberrant in ~70% | Variable | Variable | Variable | Variable | Variable |

CK7+/CK20–/CDX2– is the most common PDAC immunophenotype. Loss of SMAD4 (DPC4) is a specific PDAC marker that helps distinguish from cholangiocarcinoma in equivocal liver-mass biopsies. Maspin, IMP3, S100P, mesothelin, claudin-4, and pVHL panels are sometimes added when conventional markers are equivocal.

### 6.4 Histologic Subtypes and Grade

Conventional PDAC is graded G1 (well-differentiated, >95% glands), G2 (moderately differentiated, 50–95%), or G3 (poorly differentiated, <50%). Several variant subtypes affect prognosis:

- **Adenosquamous carcinoma** (~1–4%) — squamous component >30%; worse prognosis.
- **Colloid (mucinous noncystic) carcinoma** — better prognosis; often arises from intestinal-type IPMN.
- **Medullary carcinoma** — rare; associated with MMR deficiency / Lynch.
- **Undifferentiated** (anaplastic, sarcomatoid) — very poor prognosis.
- **Acinar cell carcinoma** — distinct entity; younger patients; sometimes lipase hypersecretion syndrome.

## 7. Molecular Workup at Diagnosis

The molecular workup of PDAC in 2026 is shaped by three landmark realities: (1) **>90% of PDAC has a KRAS mutation**, and KRAS itself is now therapeutically targetable in subsets, (2) **~10–15% of PDAC arises in a germline-mutation background**, with direct therapeutic and family-cascade implications, and (3) **a small but clinically critical fraction (~5–10%)** has a wild-type KRAS background containing actionable fusions or other targetable alterations.

### 7.1 Germline Testing — Universal, Per NCCN

NCCN has recommended **universal germline testing for every patient with confirmed pancreatic adenocarcinoma since 2019**, regardless of age, family history, or stage. The minimum panel includes:

- **BRCA1, BRCA2, PALB2** — HR repair; PARP-inhibitor eligibility (olaparib post-platinum maintenance per POLO).
- **ATM** — most prevalent hereditary PDAC predisposition gene by absolute count (~2.3% of PDAC).
- **CDKN2A** — FAMMM / p16-Leiden.
- **STK11** — Peutz-Jeghers.
- **TP53** — Li-Fraumeni.
- **MMR genes (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2, EPCAM)** — Lynch syndrome; MSI-high tumors eligible for pembrolizumab.
- **PRSS1, SPINK1, CFTR** — hereditary pancreatitis.

Real-world adherence to NCCN germline testing in PDAC remains under 40% in many systems — a substantial gap given that **~15% of PDAC has a germline driver**, and identification has direct treatment consequences and cascade implications for family members. (See document 11 for surveillance protocols.)

### 7.2 Tumor Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS)

Tumor NGS is recommended for **all patients with metastatic, locally advanced, or recurrent PDAC** and is increasingly being performed at diagnosis for patients headed to neoadjuvant therapy. A comprehensive panel reports:

- **KRAS mutation status** — codon 12 mutations: G12D (~40%), G12V (~29%), G12R (~15%), G12C (~1–3%); codon 13 and 61 rare. G12C is targetable with sotorasib or adagrasib; G12D is now targetable with the recent-generation MRTX1133 and similar inhibitors entering trials.
- **Wild-type KRAS PDAC (~5–10%)** — enriched for actionable alterations: **NRG1 fusions** (up to 20% of KRAS-WT; pharmacologic ERBB inhibition with zenocutuzumab is highly effective), **NTRK fusions** (larotrectinib, entrectinib), **RET fusions** (selpercatinib, pralsetinib), **ALK** and **ROS1 fusions**, **BRAF V600E** (encorafenib + cetuximab), **FGFR2 fusions** (pemigatinib). Total fusion prevalence in KRAS-WT PDAC reaches ~30% in some series.
- **MSI / MMR status** — MSI-high or dMMR (<1% of PDAC but extremely consequential) → pembrolizumab eligibility.
- **TMB (tumor mutational burden)** — high TMB (≥10 mut/Mb) is rare in PDAC outside MSI-high cases but can trigger checkpoint-inhibitor consideration.
- **HRD (homologous-recombination deficiency)** — somatic and germline events in HR pathway genes (BRCA1/2, PALB2, ATM, CHEK2, RAD51 family); platinum and PARPi sensitivity.
- **TP53, SMAD4, CDKN2A status** — co-occurring driver mutations are present in most PDACs; prognostic but not yet directly targetable.

### 7.3 What's in KRAS-Wild-Type PDAC

KRAS wild-type PDAC is the highest-yield molecular subset because targetable alterations are concentrated there. In KRAS-WT cases, the most common findings (variable by series) include:

| Alteration | Frequency in KRAS-WT PDAC | Targeted therapy |
|---|---|---|
| NRG1 fusion | ~10–20% | Zenocutuzumab (anti-HER2/3 bispecific) |
| BRAF V600E or non-V600E | ~5–10% | Encorafenib + cetuximab; trametinib for class II/III |
| NTRK1/2/3 fusion | ~3–5% | Larotrectinib, entrectinib |
| RET fusion | ~2–4% | Selpercatinib, pralsetinib |
| FGFR2 fusion | ~3–5% | Pemigatinib, futibatinib |
| ALK fusion | ~1–3% | Alectinib, lorlatinib |
| ROS1 fusion | ~1–2% | Crizotinib, entrectinib |
| HER2 amplification | ~2% | Trastuzumab combinations |
| MMR deficiency / MSI-H | Enriched (~5%) | Pembrolizumab |

Because these alterations are individually rare but collectively meaningful, **NGS on every KRAS-WT PDAC tumor is now considered standard**, and the molecular workup directly determines whether the patient receives standard chemotherapy or a targeted agent.

## 8. AJCC 8th Edition TNM Staging

The AJCC 8th edition (effective January 2018) was the first significant overhaul of pancreatic-cancer staging since 1997, and the changes are mostly conceptual improvements aimed at distributing patients more evenly across stages and improving prognostic discrimination.

### 8.1 T Classification — Tumor

| Stage | 7th edition | 8th edition |
|---|---|---|
| Tis | Carcinoma in situ | Carcinoma in situ (PanIN-3, HG IPMN, HG MCN) |
| T1 | Limited to pancreas, ≤2 cm | ≤2 cm (further: T1a ≤0.5 cm; T1b 0.5–1 cm; T1c 1–2 cm) |
| T2 | Limited to pancreas, >2 cm | >2 cm and ≤4 cm |
| T3 | Extends beyond pancreas, no celiac/SMA | >4 cm |
| T4 | Involves celiac axis or SMA (unresectable) | Tumor involves celiac axis, SMA, and/or common hepatic artery (unresectability designation removed) |

The major shift is to **size-only criteria for T1–T3** (away from "extension beyond pancreas," which was largely subjective and present in most resected cases) and the **removal of the "unresectable" designation from T4**, recognizing that resectability is a separate, treatment-context-dependent judgment now informed by neoadjuvant response.

### 8.2 N Classification — Nodes

| Stage | 7th edition | 8th edition |
|---|---|---|
| N0 | No regional nodes | No regional nodes |
| N1 | Regional nodes positive | 1–3 regional nodes positive |
| N2 | — | ≥4 regional nodes positive |

Splitting N1 by nodal burden was a key prognostic upgrade — patients with 1–3 vs ≥4 positive nodes have meaningfully different survival, and this is now reflected.

### 8.3 M Classification — Metastases

M0 (no distant metastasis) vs M1 (distant metastasis present) — unchanged.

### 8.4 Stage Grouping

| Stage | TNM |
|---|---|
| 0 | Tis N0 M0 |
| IA | T1 N0 M0 |
| IB | T2 N0 M0 |
| IIA | T3 N0 M0 |
| IIB | T1–T3 N1 M0 |
| III | Any T N2 M0; or T4 Any N M0 |
| IV | Any T Any N M1 |

The 8th edition produces a more balanced stage distribution. In a representative series, 7th-edition staging classified ~87% of resected patients as pT3, collapsing prognostic discrimination; 8th-edition staging redistributes them across T1 (27%), T2 (57%), T3 (16%), yielding genuinely different survival curves by stage. International validation studies have confirmed modestly improved prognostic accuracy.

### 8.5 Stage Migration with Neoadjuvant Therapy

A subtle but important issue in 2026 practice: as more patients with borderline-resectable or even resectable disease are now treated with **neoadjuvant chemotherapy** before surgery, the resected pathologic stage no longer reflects untreated biology. Neoadjuvant therapy can sterilize nodes (ypN0 in patients who would have been pN1), shrink primary tumors (yp T downstaging), and rarely produce a complete pathologic response (~5% ypT0N0 with FOLFIRINOX-based regimens). This is real benefit, not an artifact, but it makes comparison with historical (upfront-surgery) series difficult and complicates use of pathologic stage for adjuvant-therapy decisions. The "ypTNM" designation (y = post-treatment, p = pathologic) is the correct nomenclature for resected post-neoadjuvant specimens.

## 9. NCCN Resectability Categories

Beyond TNM staging, the **NCCN resectability framework** is the practical tool that determines whether a patient goes to surgery first, gets neoadjuvant therapy, or is treated as advanced disease. It is grounded in anatomic relationships between the tumor and the SMA, celiac axis, common hepatic artery, SMV, and portal vein on high-quality pancreatic-protocol CT.

### 9.1 Resectability Categories — 2025 NCCN

| Category | Arterial involvement | Venous involvement | Distant disease | Typical management |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Resectable** | No tumor contact with SMA, CA, or CHA | No tumor contact with SMV/PV, or contact ≤180° **without** vein contour irregularity | None | Upfront surgery or neoadjuvant per institutional preference; adjuvant FOLFIRINOX |
| **Borderline resectable** | Tumor contact with CHA without extension to CA bifurcation or hepatic artery branches; tumor contact with SMA ≤180°; **head/uncinate**: solid contact with CA ≤180° (some criteria) | Tumor contact with SMV/PV >180°, or contact ≤180° with contour irregularity or thrombosis but reconstructable; solid contact with IVC | None | Neoadjuvant chemotherapy ± chemoradiation, then surgical reassessment |
| **Locally advanced (unresectable)** | Tumor contact with SMA >180°; contact with CA >180°; contact with first jejunal SMA branch; aortic involvement | Unreconstructable SMV/PV due to tumor or bland thrombus | None | Induction systemic therapy; some converted to resection after response (~20%) |
| **Metastatic** | Any | Any | Liver, peritoneal, lung, distant nodes | Palliative systemic therapy; no role for resection |

### 9.2 Vascular Anatomy in Plain Language

- **Superior mesenteric artery (SMA):** Comes off the aorta just below the celiac axis, immediately behind the uncinate process of the pancreas. SMA involvement is the *most* feared vascular finding because the SMA supplies the small bowel and right colon; reconstruction is hazardous, and post-resection survival benefit is modest. Many borderline / locally advanced patients fall into these categories specifically because of SMA contact.
- **Celiac axis (CA):** Comes off the aorta just above the SMA; supplies the stomach, liver, and spleen. CA involvement excludes standard pancreatoduodenectomy; for body-tail tumors, the **modified Appleby (distal pancreatectomy with celiac axis resection)** is a salvage option but requires intact collateral hepatic flow.
- **Common hepatic artery (CHA):** Branches off the celiac axis to the liver; ≤180° contact in head tumors is reconstructable and usually borderline rather than unresectable.
- **Superior mesenteric vein (SMV) and portal vein (PV):** Both lie immediately behind the pancreatic neck/head. Tumor contact, narrowing, or even short-segment occlusion is *routinely* reconstructable (graft or end-to-end anastomosis) at experienced centers — venous involvement alone, without arterial contact, is typically borderline-resectable and not a hard contraindication.
- **Splenic vein:** Runs along the dorsal pancreas to join the SMV at the portal confluence. Occluded splenic vein is a marker of body/tail tumors and sometimes drives sinistral portal hypertension.

### 9.3 Staging Laparoscopy — When and Why

Even after maximal cross-sectional imaging, **a meaningful minority of "resectable" patients are found at laparoscopy to have radiologically occult peritoneal or liver-surface metastases**, particularly when the primary tumor is large or biology is aggressive. The yield (positive laparoscopy / total) is approximately:

- Resectable disease on imaging: ~5–14%.
- Borderline-resectable: ~15–25%.
- Locally advanced: ~30–50%.

NCCN and most centers consider staging laparoscopy in patients with one or more of the following risk factors: **CA 19-9 > 150 (some series use 500) U/mL**, tumor size > 3 cm, body/tail location, indeterminate extrapancreatic lesion on imaging, low-volume ascites, or suspicious lymphadenopathy. Peritoneal washings for cytology (and increasingly for CEA + CA 19-9) can be done at the same procedure; cytology-positive disease is treated as M1, even without visible metastases.

## 10. Precursor Lesions — IPMN, MCN, PanIN

PDAC arises through a recognized series of precursor lesions, each with distinct clinical, radiologic, and management implications. The clinical management of mucinous precursors is now framed by the **2024 Kyoto guidelines** (the latest update of the 2017 International Association of Pancreatology / Fukuoka guidelines).

### 10.1 PanIN — Pancreatic Intraepithelial Neoplasia

PanIN is the **most common precursor lesion of PDAC** and is a microscopic finding in pancreatic ducts. It is not visualized on imaging — it is detected only in resection specimens (or rarely on cytology). Current grading is binary:

- **Low-grade PanIN (LG PanIN)** — formerly PanIN-1 and PanIN-2. Common incidental finding in adults; rarely clinically significant in isolation.
- **High-grade PanIN (HG PanIN)** — formerly PanIN-3 (carcinoma in situ). Immediate precursor to invasive PDAC.

Genetic progression mirrors morphology: **KRAS mutation and telomere shortening are earliest** (LG PanIN); **CDKN2A loss is intermediate**; **TP53 and SMAD4 inactivation are late, in HG PanIN and invasive disease**. Because PanIN is not imaging-detectable, it is essentially never a clinical management problem on its own — but in patients with high familial / germline risk under EUS surveillance, the finding of an indeterminate lesion is often correlated with underlying PanIN at autopsy or resection.

### 10.2 IPMN — Intraductal Papillary Mucinous Neoplasm

IPMN is a mucinous, intraductal precursor lesion that **is visible on imaging as a cystic lesion in communication with the main pancreatic duct or a side branch**. Subtypes:

- **Main-duct IPMN (MD-IPMN)** — diffuse or segmental dilation of the main pancreatic duct ≥5 mm. **High risk of malignancy (40–60% by resection); surgical resection generally recommended.**
- **Branch-duct IPMN (BD-IPMN)** — cystic dilation of one or more side branches, with the main duct preserved. **Lower risk (~15–25% in surgical series, much lower in surveillance series); often managed conservatively with surveillance.**
- **Mixed-type IPMN** — features of both; managed as MD-IPMN.

Histologic differentiation subtypes (gastric, intestinal, pancreatobiliary, oncocytic — replaced by "oncocytic" being its own entity in WHO 2022) carry distinct prognoses; intestinal-type IPMN tends toward colloid carcinoma (better prognosis); pancreatobiliary tends toward tubular adenocarcinoma (worse).

### 10.3 Kyoto 2024 / Fukuoka Updates — High-Risk Stigmata and Worrisome Features

The Kyoto 2024 guidelines retain the Fukuoka two-tier framework but refine the criteria, increase the emphasis on EUS (including contrast-enhanced EUS), introduce cyst growth-rate criteria, and refine post-resection surveillance:

**High-Risk Stigmata (HRS) — surgical resection indicated:**

- Obstructive jaundice with cystic lesion in pancreatic head.
- Enhancing mural nodule ≥5 mm.
- Main pancreatic duct ≥10 mm.
- Cytology positive or suspicious for malignancy.

**Worrisome Features (WF) — EUS evaluation and close follow-up; surgery considered:**

- Cyst ≥3 cm.
- Enhancing mural nodule <5 mm.
- Thickened/enhancing cyst walls.
- Main pancreatic duct 5–9.9 mm.
- Abrupt change in caliber of pancreatic duct with distal pancreatic atrophy.
- Lymphadenopathy.
- Elevated serum CA 19-9.
- Cyst growth rate **≥2.5 mm/year** (new in Kyoto 2024 — replaces less-specific prior thresholds).
- **New-onset or recent exacerbation of diabetes mellitus** (new in Kyoto 2024).
- Acute pancreatitis caused by the IPMN.

EUS-FNA cyst-fluid analysis now incorporates **CEA (>192 ng/mL → mucinous), amylase (high → ductal communication), cytology, and molecular markers (KRAS, GNAS mutations → IPMN; VHL → serous cystadenoma; CTNNB1 → solid pseudopapillary tumor; TP53 / SMAD4 / CDKN2A → high-grade dysplasia or invasive cancer)**. KRAS-mutation status in cyst fluid is independently associated with non-indolent behavior.

### 10.4 MCN — Mucinous Cystic Neoplasm

MCN is a mucinous cyst that **does not communicate with the pancreatic duct**, occurs almost exclusively in women (typically 40–60 years), is most often in the body or tail, and is defined histologically by an ovarian-type stroma. Because the malignancy risk is appreciable (~10–25%) and MCNs occur in younger patients with long survival ahead, **surgical resection is generally recommended** for any MCN >3 cm or with worrisome features. Smaller (<3 cm), uncomplicated MCNs may be surveilled in elderly or comorbid patients.

### 10.5 Pancreatic Cyst Management — Practical Summary

| Cyst type | Communication with duct | Cyst fluid CEA | Cyst fluid amylase | Typical management |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Pseudocyst** | Variable | Low | **Very high** | Drainage if symptomatic; no malignant potential |
| **Serous cystadenoma** | No | Low | Low | Surveillance; benign (rare exception) |
| **Branch-duct IPMN** | Yes | High (>192) | High | Kyoto criteria; surveillance vs. resect |
| **Main-duct IPMN** | Yes (main duct ≥5 mm) | High | High | Generally resect |
| **MCN** | No | High | Low–intermediate | Resect if >3 cm or symptoms |
| **Solid pseudopapillary tumor** | No | Variable | Variable | Resect; young women; favorable prognosis |
| **Cystic NET** | No | Low | Low | Resect or surveil per size + functional status |

## 11. Where Compute Could Help

A self-funded volunteer-compute project oriented at PDAC can do real, peer-reviewable work at the diagnostic-and-staging stage of disease — and *especially* at the early-detection end, where the field is bottlenecked by underpowered biomarker discovery and the absence of integrated risk models. Tagged with [A] for direct compute angles:

- **[A1] Lewis-negative-tolerant biomarker discovery.** ~10% of patients are CA 19-9 blind. A volunteer-compute pipeline can systematically reanalyze public PDAC proteomic, glycoproteomic, methylation, and cfDNA datasets stratified by Lewis status, train classifiers in Lewis-negative subsets, and externally validate. Compute task = feature-selection / classifier-training over high-dimensional small-N data with cross-cohort validation; embarrassingly parallel.

- **[A2] Multi-modal early-detection classifier benchmarking.** CancerSEEK, Galleri, IMMray PanCan-d, MUC5AC/MUC16, TIMP1/LRG1 panels have all been published but never head-to-head benchmarked on the same patient set. A federated, public-dataset-driven benchmark — running identical preprocessing and classifier-training code via BOINC tasks against each published feature set — would clarify which panels generalize.

- **[A3] Multi-omic integration of NOD + biomarkers + imaging features.** New-onset diabetes is an ~6–10× risk concentrator over baseline. Combining NOD (END-PAC and successor scores) with serum biomarkers, age, sex, ethnicity, and incidental imaging features could lift the population PPV from ~1% to a level where targeted screening becomes economical. Compute task = grid-search of multi-omic integration architectures (logistic / random-forest / gradient-boosted / transformer-based) over heterogeneous public datasets.

- **[A4] Pancreatic-CT radiomics for sub-cm tumor detection.** Pancreatic-protocol CT misses ~20–30% of <2 cm PDACs. Public datasets (PANORAMA, MSD Pancreas, PANDA challenge) contain thousands of pancreatic CTs with annotation. Volunteer compute can run 3D-CNN / nnU-Net training, hyperparameter search, and augmentation experiments at the scale academic labs cannot afford, evaluated against the PANORAMA hidden test set.

- **[A5] Imaging-detection of pre-invasive lesions.** PanIN is microscopic; HG-PanIN and small main-duct IPMN can produce subtle ductal-caliber and parenchymal-atrophy changes detectable on CT or MRI before frank tumor appears. Training models on serial pre-diagnostic imaging from large EHR cohorts (e.g., PANC-EARLY, UCSF PRECEDE) would yield a "tumor-prequel" detector. Compute task = self-supervised pretraining over millions of incidental abdominal scans.

- **[A6] Open-source CA 19-9-replacement signature on cell-free DNA fragmentomics.** Galleri-type methylation classifiers are commercial and opaque. An open-source, volunteer-trained methylation + fragmentation-pattern classifier with published model weights would lower the bar for hospital systems and research labs to deploy population-screening pilots.

- **[A7] Cyst fluid multi-omic classifier for malignant transformation.** IPMN-vs-PDAC discrimination from cyst-fluid proteins, mutations (KRAS / GNAS / TP53 / SMAD4 / CDKN2A), methylation, and microbiome is currently fragmented across small studies. Federated retraining and benchmarking with volunteer compute would produce a single deployable classifier.

- **[A8] Refined END-PAC and NOD-risk modeling on EHR data.** New-onset-diabetes-based detection is the single most actionable opportunistic screening signal in PDAC. Public claims and EHR datasets (MIMIC, All-of-Us, UK Biobank, CPRD) are large enough to train far better discrimination than current END-PAC. Compute task = survival modeling / sequential prediction over very long time series.

- **[A9] Resectability and vascular-involvement assessment standardization.** Interobserver agreement on NCCN resectability categories — especially borderline — is only fair to moderate. A computer-vision pipeline trained on public CT datasets to quantify the *exact* circumferential degree of contact between tumor and SMA / CA / CHA / SMV / PV (rather than human-eye estimates) would standardize the most consequential treatment-stratifying assessment in the field.

- **[A10] Cachexia trajectory modeling from serial imaging + labs.** L3-skeletal-muscle area, myosteatosis density, and visceral adiposity can be automatically segmented from any abdominal CT. Volunteer compute can extract these from large public CT archives, link them to outcomes, and produce predictive models of cachexia trajectory and chemotherapy tolerance — directly informing prehabilitation and early-PERT decisions.

The common architecture: **federated, embarrassingly parallel, public-dataset-anchored, fully open-source models with public weights, evaluated against held-out benchmarks**. This is exactly the niche BOINC-style volunteer compute can fill while academic groups remain constrained by single-institution data and commercial groups remain closed-source.
